## II. The Representation of Projective Residuals

To compute with exact remainders without incurring unbounded memory overhead, we define the algebraic representation of the projective residual $r_z$ as a hybrid structure.

Let $S = \varprojlim S_i$ be an infinite profinite set. At a designated zoom level $z \in I$, the truncated state is $x_z \in Q(S_z)$. We define the **algebraic remainder** $r_z$ as the exact projective residual:

$$r_z = x - x_z \in \mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$$

We represent this residual using a **Hybrid Rational-Power Series Model**:

At the application level ($QnLang$), the remainder is represented as an exact, symbolic coprime rational fraction in the $p$-adic field:

$$r_z = \frac{a}{b} \in \mathbb{Q}_p$$

At the compiler level ($QnIR$), the remainder is compiled into a lazy-evaluated $p$-adic power series:

$$r_z = \sum_{i=z+1}^{\infty} a_i p^i$$

where each $a_i \in \{0, 1, \dots, p-1\}$ is a $p$-adic coefficient.

This hybrid structure ensures that the remainder is manipulated as a compact, exact fraction during standard computations [3], but remains expandable into a sequential power series only when a deeper zoom level ($z+m$) is requested, minimizing register allocation overhead [4].

## III. Complexity Bounds and Algebraic Simplification Filters

To prevent the denominator $b$ of the rational remainder $r_z = \frac{a}{b}$ from experiencing exponential bit-width expansion during nested arithmetic, the system must enforce strict algebraic constraints.

### A. The Multiplication Expansion
Let $r_{1} = \frac{a_1}{b_1}$ and $r_{2} = \frac{a_2}{b_2}$ be the remainders of two $Qn$ states. Their product results in:

$$r_{1} \cdot r_{2} = \frac{a_1 a_2}{b_1 b_2}$$

Without intervention, the bit-width of the new denominator $b_1 b_2$ is the sum of the bit-widths of $b_1$ and $b_2$, doubling the memory footprint of the remainder with every subsequent multiplication [3].

### B. Algebraic Simplification Filters
To bound this growth, the $QnIR$ compilation target implements **Algebraic Simplification Filters**. Immediately following any algebraic operation on the remainders, the runtime executes a greatest common divisor (GCD) reduction using the **Schönhage Fast GCD algorithm** [6, 7]:

$$d = \text{GCD}(a_1 a_2, \; b_1 b_2) \implies r_{\text{new}} = \frac{(a_1 a_2)/d}{(b_1 b_2)/d}$$

Under the complexity bounds of fast computer algebra, this reduction is computed in:

$$O(M(z \log p) \log z)$$

time, where $M(n)$ is the complexity of multiplying two $n$-bit integers [3, 6]. 

By executing this fast reduction at the register level, the system ensures that common factors are instantly canceled out. This restricts the bit-width of the remainder to a stable, linear growth rate $O(z)$ rather than an exponential rate $O(2^z)$, preserving the comfortable execution timeline of the processor [3, 5].

### A. The Hard Halt and DVFS Scaling (Substrate Protection)
The **Hard Halt** is a physical, resource-bounded termination enforced by the local **Resource Functor** $\Phi$ during JIT compilation [12]:
*   **The Trigger:** It is triggered when the execution of the active thread reaches the maximum allowable zoom level $z_{\max}$ defined by the hardware capacity, or when the expected idle window is mathematically proven to exceed the **Thermal Stability Window ($\mathcal{T}_{\text{thermal}}$)**:
    $$t_{\text{idle}} \ge \mathcal{T}_{\text{thermal}}$$
*   **The Execution:** The `cdqnK` kernel halts the active register computation loop and power-gates the core into deep sleep (C-states), cutting off static leakage current [12]. The uncomputed fractional residual is safely written to disk as the exact remainder $r_{\max} \in Q_{\text{disk}}$.
*   **The DVFS Mitigation:** If the expected idle window is short ($t_{\text{idle}} < \mathcal{T}_{\text{thermal}}$), entering deep sleep is strictly blocked [13]. The scheduler dynamically down-scales the operating voltage and frequency (DVFS / P-states) [12]. This reduces dynamic power consumption while keeping the core running continuously at a cool, balanced temperature, minimizing the temporal temperature oscillation magnitude:
    $$\Delta T = T_{\text{active}} - T_{\text{idle}}$$
    According to the Coffin-Manson equation used in semiconductor structural reliability, the number of cycles to failure ($N_f$) is inversely proportional to $\Delta T$ raised to an exponent $q \ge 2$ [13]:
    $$N_f \propto \left( \frac{1}{\Delta T} \right)^q$$
    By keeping $\Delta T \to 0$ via continuous DVFS scaling, the compiler structurally prevents micro-fractures in solder joints and metallization traces [13].

### B. The Soft Halt (Natural Termination)
The **Soft Halt** is a logical, algebraic termination enforced by the algebraic state of the remainder:
*   **The Trigger:** It is triggered when the projective residual $r_z$ collapses to exactly zero:
    $$r_z = x - x_z = 0$$
*   **The Decidability:** Determining whether $r_z = 0$ is known in computer algebra as the **Zero-Equivalence Problem**, which is generally undecidable for transcendental functions. However, because $r_z = \frac{a}{b}$ is represented as an exact coprime rational fraction, `cdqnK` solves the Zero-Equivalence Problem in $O(1)$ constant time by reducing the fraction to its **canonical normal form** and testing if the numerator $a$ is exactly zero [8, 9]:
    $$a = 0 \implies r_z = 0$$
    This prevents uncomputable halting states and ensures decidability at the register level [8].
*   **The Execution:** If $r_z = 0$, the calculation has converged to an exact, absolute, and stable limit. At this point, continuing to execute the loop would result in redundant operations ($0 \oplus 0 \oplus \dots$), wasting CPU clock cycles and dissipating heat. The `cdqnK` kernel immediately detects the zero-numerator state, bypasses all subsequent operations via branch-free stack selection, and triggers a **Soft Halt** **`[empirical horizon]`** [14, 15]. The register loop is terminated naturally. This saves physical clock cycles, decreases memory bandwidth consumption, and maximizes the energy efficiency of the SoC [4].

This halting continuous model ensures that the system is bounded symmetrically: the Hard Halt/DVFS scaling protects the physical limits of the hardware, while the Soft Halt optimizes the logical limits of the algebra.

## VI. Glossary of Terms for Non-Technical Readers

*   **Projective Residual (Remainder):** The exact difference between an infinite-precision number and its finite, truncated representation on a computer. It represents the uncomputed "tail" of the calculation.
*   **Greatest Common Divisor (GCD):** The largest positive integer that divides two numbers without leaving a remainder, used here to simplify fractions and prevent memory bloating.
*   **Hard Halt:** A forced termination of a calculation when the computer's physical memory or register limits are reached. It saves the uncompleted part of the calculation to be resumed later.
*   **Soft Halt:** A natural termination of a calculation when the exact mathematical result is reached (the remainder is zero). It stops the processor from wasting energy on redundant steps.
*   **Zero-Equivalence Problem:** An algorithmic algebra problem of proving whether a given mathematical expression is equal to zero.
*   **Canonical Normal Form:** A standardized mathematical format that represents an algebraic object uniquely, ensuring that identical objects always have the exact same representation.
*   **Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS / P-states):** A hardware-level technique that dynamically down-scales the operating voltage and frequency of a processor core to match its real-time workload, flattening temperature oscillations ($\Delta T$) to prevent physical Thermal Cycling (TC) fatigue.
*   **Power Gating (C-states):** A power-saving technique that completely cuts off the supply voltage to an inactive processor core, eliminating both dynamic switching and static leakage current during long idle periods.
