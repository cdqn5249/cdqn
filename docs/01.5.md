### Abstract
This paper establishes the fifth step in the CDQN-01 series by proposing the mathematical and category-theoretic formulation of bidirectional scaling for the Quang number ($Qn$) primitive. Building upon the $p$-adic ultrametric framework in Chapter 1.4, we address the problem of structural fragmentation between microscopic register-level calculations and macroscopic continuous fields. We define the Ind-Pro category completion ($\mathbf{Ind}\text{-}\mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$) to unify totally disconnected, discrete Pro-limits ($\varprojlim$) with continuous, inductive Ind-limits ($\varinjlim$). Furthermore, we construct the Spectral Sequence of Scales ($E_r$) to formally model how algebraic constraints and physical capabilities scale dynamically, providing the mathematical foundation for hardware-adaptive performance calibration. This dual-limit architecture serves as the multi-scale scaling mechanism of the cdqn ecosystem.

## II. The Ind-Pro Category Completion

To allow the $Qn$ primitive to scale seamlessly across microscopic and macroscopic boundaries, we define its algebraic representation over the dual completions of our context category.

### A. Filtered Limits and Colimits
Let $\mathcal{C}$ be the category of finite, discrete context spaces. To model infinite scaling, we must complete this category under both filtered limits (to zoom in) and filtered colimits (to zoom out).

An inductive system in $\mathcal{C}$ is a functor from a filtered index category to $\mathcal{C}$, representing a sequence of scaling inclusions [1]. A projective system in $\mathcal{C}$ is a functor from the opposite of a filtered index category to $\mathcal{C}$, representing a sequence of refining projections [1].

### B. The Ind-Pro Category
Following the formalizations of Masaki Kashiwara and Pierre Schapira, we define the **Pro-completion** $\mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$ as the category of projective limits of objects in $\mathcal{C}$, and the **Ind-completion** $\mathbf{Ind}(\mathcal{C})$ as the category of inductive colimits of objects in $\mathcal{C}$ [1].

To unify both directions under a single algebraic framework, we define the **Ind-Pro-completion** of $\mathcal{C}$ as:

$$\mathbf{Ind}\text{-}\mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C}) = \mathbf{Ind}\left(\mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})\right)$$

This category contains both arbitrary filtered limits and colimits. It allows us to represent any multi-scale coordinate state $X \in \mathbf{Ind}\text{-}\mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$ as a double limit of finite discrete spaces, establishing the mathematical setting where continuous and discrete states coexist [1, 2].

## III. The Adjoint Functor Pair (The Ind-Pro Map)

Using the Ind-Pro category completion, we construct the formal mathematical operators that transition between the microscopic and macroscopic scales.

### A. The Projective Functor (Zooming In)
When the system zooms in to resolve microscopic precision (approaching $z \to -\infty$), the Zoom functor $z$ acts as a projective operator. It maps the continuous space to a Pro-object, which is represented as the inverse limit of finite discrete quotients:

$$S = \varprojlim S_i \in \mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$$

This inverse limit maps the space to a totally disconnected profinite set (resembling Cantor dust or $p$-adic integers). The behavior at this scale is dominated by discrete, non-local transitions and cohomological obstructions ($H^1 \neq 0$).

### B. The Inductive Functor (Zooming Out)
When the system zooms out to resolve macroscopic scales (approaching $z \to +\infty$), the Zoom functor $z$ acts as an inductive operator. It maps the discrete, localized quotients to an Ind-object, which is represented as the direct (inductive) limit of inclusions:

$$V = \varinjlim V_i \in \mathbf{Ind}(\mathcal{C})$$

This direct limit glues the disconnected, discrete local states together into a continuous macroscopic solid vector space. The discrete local obstructions are integrated and smoothed out, allowing continuous, smooth geometric fields (such as gravitational geometry or smooth spatial manifolds) to emerge natively from the underlying discrete registers.

### C. The Cohesive Adjoint Pair & Functorial State Reuse
The transition between these two scaling directions is managed by a cohesive adjoint functor pair $(\mathbb{L}, \; \mathbb{R})$:

$$\mathbb{L}: \mathbf{Pro}(\mathcal{C}) \rightleftarrows \mathbf{Ind}(\mathcal{C}) : \mathbb{R}$$

Because the Pro-limit is represented as a coherent, persistent sequence of approximations, the truncated state at depth $z$ is not a destructive truncation. The state natively carries its current Zoom $z$ precision level and its exact, uncomputed algebraic projective residual (the remainder $r_z$) as part of its type. 

If the user or runtime subsequently requires a deeper precision ($z \to z+m$), the compiler ($QnLang$) does not recompute the sequence from scratch ($z=0$). It uses the adjoint functor $\mathbb{L} \dashv \mathbb{R}$ to read the carried state $(x_z, r_z)$ and resumes computation exactly from step $z+1$. This establishes a non-destructive, fully reusable, and incremental scaling loop.

## IV. The Spectral Sequence of Scales and Hardware Calibration

To execute these multi-scale computations on physical hardware, we must formally model how the algebraic constraints ($dx = 0$) and physical capabilities ($\Phi$) change dynamically across the scale poset $I$.

### A. The Spectral Sequence Formulation
We construct the scale-dependent filtering of constraints using a **Spectral Sequence** [3]. Let $C^\bullet$ be a filtered cochain complex of sheaves on the site of profinite sets $\mathbf{Prof}$, where the filtration:

$$F_z C^\bullet \subseteq F_{z-1} C^\bullet \subseteq \dots$$

is indexed by the Zoom scale $z \in I$ [14]. This filtration induces a spectral sequence of abelian groups $E_r^{p,q}$ (called pages) equipped with differential operators [3, 14]:

$$d_r: E_r^{p,q} \to E_r^{p+r, \; q-r+1}$$

that satisfy the boundary condition $d_r \circ d_r = 0$.

Under this homological mapping, the filtration degree corresponds to the Zoom scale $z \in I$, while the page $r$ represents the successive stages of homological boundary resolution [14]. The algebraic constraints and security bounds ($dx = 0$) are filtered dynamically across these pages $E_r$, ensuring that the rules of one scale do not leak and destabilize another.

### B. Hardware-Adaptive Calibration
During the bootstrap phase, the **cdqnOS** kernel evaluates the local **Resource Functor** $\Phi$ to profile the physical capabilities of the local hardware (such as memory wall bandwidth and register sizes) [4]:

$$\Phi: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{H}$$

Following the formalisms of **Linear Logic** and **Linear Type Systems**, physical resources (CPU registers, memory cells) are treated as non-duplicable, consumable assets [15, 16]. The functor $\Phi$ uses this resource-bounded model to statically calculate the maximum stable spectral sequence page $r$ that the device can compute in linear time:

$$r_{\max} = z_{\max}$$

This prevents memory overflows and register exhaustion by construction [16].

**Low-Precision Daily Tasks:**  
For standard human tasks (such as currency transactions), a deep zoom level is a waste of processing energy and registers. In base 10, currency only requires $z \approx 3$. The cdqnOS kernel detects this active context and dynamically clamps the execution zoom, shutting down redundant high-precision register lanes and saving CPU clock cycles.

**High-Precision 3D Graphics:**  
Conversely, 3D graphics require deep precision to prevent spatial coordinate distortion. Rendering a large virtual world at a low resolution like $z = 3$ causes geometric models to snap and jitter. The system increases the local scale to $z = 32$ or $z = 64$ (single or double precision) to maintain rendering exactness across wide virtual terrains [8].

### C. Register Boundaries vs. Theoretical Limits
The $Qn$ primitive itself has no mathematical limit; its algebraic formulation allows $z$ to approach infinity ($z \to \infty$) over the infinite projective limit. However, physical hardware execution is bounded by the register bus width of the local processor:

**Single-Cycle Hardware Execution ($z \le 128$):**  
On a 128-bit vector execution register file (such as ARM Neon or Intel AVX), any $2$-adic algebraic operation at zoom level $z \le 128$ is processed natively by the hardware ALU in exactly one CPU clock cycle.

**Multi-Cycle Software Execution ($z > 128$):**  
If a specialized task requires a precision exceeding the register width (e.g., $z = 512$ on a 128-bit bus), the cdqnOS kernel automatically executes multi-precision software arithmetic [9]. The compiler ($QnIR$) splits the calculation into a sequence of four 128-bit register operations. This remains mathematically exact but scales the execution time over multiple clock cycles.

## VI. Glossary of Terms for Non-Technical Readers

*   **Filtered Colimit (Ind-Limit):** A mathematical process of constructing a larger, continuous universe by gluing smaller, local mathematical structures together in a directed, non-overlapping sequence.
*   **Filtered Limit (Pro-Limit):** A mathematical process of constructing an infinite-precision microscopic space by nesting simpler, finite spaces inside one another and tracing the paths through them.
*   **Ind-Pro Category:** A unified algebraic category that contains both infinite filtered limits (for microscopic details) and infinite filtered colimits (for macroscopic scales) under a single mathematical system.
*   **Adjoint Functor Pair:** A pair of mappings between categories that work in opposite directions and are linked by a strict mathematical symmetry, ensuring that translating data back and forth preserves all structural rules.
*   **Spectral Sequence:** A mathematical tool that performs calculations on a space by taking successive, layered approximations (called pages) corresponding to different scales or resolutions of the space.
*   **Resource Functor ($\Phi$):** The compiler operator in cdqnOS that maps abstract mathematical contexts to the physical capacity and performance boundaries of the local CPU and memory.
