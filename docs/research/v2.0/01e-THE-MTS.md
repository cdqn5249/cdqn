## 1. Abstract
Current Operating Systems (OS) are primarily designed as resource allocators, facilitating the execution of instructions while remaining largely indifferent to the **thermodynamic integrity** of the resulting states. This paper identifies the "Governance Constraint" of standard kernels: their inability to distinguish between high-entropy fluctuations (such as hallucinations or exploits) and low-entropy stable facts at the physical level. We propose the **Morphic Thermodynamic System (MTS)** as a complementary alternative for high-integrity environments. By introducing the **Axiom of Thermodynamic Governance**, we describe a system where the kernel functions as a **"Loom,"** regulating state-stability via entropy dissipation. This approach provides a framework for digital environments that are physically resilient to unauthorized logical drift.

## 3. The Diagnostic: Identifying the Governance Constraint

Traditional computing assumes that a secure state can be maintained through logic-layer gatekeeping (Access Control Lists). However, 2026 industrial data suggests this model faces an asymptote in high-stakes agentic environments.

### 3.1 The Limit of Software-Centric Verification
Recent evaluations of "Intrinsic Self-Critique" in large-scale models (**Bohnet et al., DeepMind, 2025**) indicate that while models can improve their own planning through iteration, they remain susceptible to "False Positives"—labeling incorrect logic as correct. 
*   **Analysis:** This suggests that software-only verification lacks a "Physical Floor." Without a connection to the substrate's energy state, self-correction remains a probabilistic exercise rather than a deterministic physical requirement.

### 3.2 The Permissions Paradox
In standard Von Neumann architectures, a "Zero-Day" exploit often succeeds because it executes at the same energy-priority as a system command. The OS manages "Who" is acting (identity), but it is physically indifferent to **"How much entropy"** the action generates. We identify this indifference as a structural vulnerability for systems requiring absolute data sovereignty and source consistency.
