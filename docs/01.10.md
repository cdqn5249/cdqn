# Chapter 1.10: Physical Unclonable Stalks ($PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}$) and the Silicon Anchor

---

### Abstract
This paper establishes the tenth step in the CDQN-01 series by proposing the mathematical and cryptographic formulation of the Physical Unclonable Stalk ($PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}$) and the Silicon Anchor. Building upon the boundary-level verification framework in Chapter 1.9, we address the problem of physical node cloning, emulation attacks, and identity leakage in decentralized networks. We define the $PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}_c$ as a composite algebraic cell bound to silicon-level latency variations and thermal noise, using fuzzy extractors to stabilize the raw physical entropy. Furthermore, we construct a Symmetric Monoidal Functor to map pairing-based Direct Anonymous Attestation (DAA) over condensed categories, proving that a node can attest its physical genuineness to the network with zero identity leakage. This hardware-software co-design serves as the physical security mechanism of the cdqn ecosystem.

---

## I. Introduction and Continuity from Chapter 1.9

In Chapter 1.9 (CDQN-01.9, v1.0.1), we formulated the **Cohomological Task Stack** and **Boundary-Level Verification** [1]. We demonstrated that the cdqnOS kernel can treat physical CPU registers as a blind algebraic black box, executing calculations at maximum physical frequency and verifying security and semantic invariants ($dx = 0$ and $H^1 = 0$) strictly at the projection boundaries (disk-to-RAM and RAM-to-disk transfers) [1].

However, this boundary-level verification introduces a critical security boundary:
*   The boundary checks assume that the physical node executing the calculations is a genuine, certified, and unaltered silicon chip.
*   If an adversary executes the $QnIR$ bytecode inside an emulated, virtualized, or cloned software environment, they can easily forge the boundary checks by mocking the hardware return states.
*   Furthermore, if the system relies on static hardware identifiers (like CPU serial numbers) for attestation, it introduces a severe privacy vulnerability, exposing the physical node and the user to permanent tracking and profiling across the network.

### The Constructive Realizability Hypothesis
In accordance with **Mathematical Constructivism**, we treat the derivations in this paper as a **Formal Design Hypothesis** [17]. We do not claim absolute mathematical truth in physical reality; rather, we are constructing a computable type-specification. The ultimate proof of this hardware-anchored security model is the successful physical execution of the compiled **cdqnK** operating system kernel written in **QnLang** running natively on physical, verified registers [17, 18].

## II. The Physical Unclonable Stalk ($PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}_c$)

To mathematically bind our abstract software sheaves to the physical identity of the executing silicon, we define the **Physical Unclonable Stalk** ($PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}_c$).

### A. The Composite On-Disk Cell
Let $Q \in \mathbf{CondSet}$ be a Quang number primitive, and let $Q_{\text{disk}}$ be its persistent representation on-disk. We represent $Q_{\text{disk}}$ as a composite algebraic cell:

$$Q_{\text{disk}} = \left( c, \; \mathcal{F}_c, \; \pi_c, \; \pi_{\text{puf}} \right)$$

Where:
*   $c$ is the context coordinate index.
*   $\mathcal{F}_c$ is the logical data payload of the stalk.
*   $\pi_c$ is the mathematical type-proof of the dependencies.
*   **$\pi_{\text{puf}}$ is the Physical Constructive Witness**, representing the unique physical identity of the executing silicon.

### B. Silicon Entropy Harvesting
The physical witness $\pi_{\text{puf}}$ is generated on-demand by harvesting the microscopic, sub-micron physical manufacturing variations of the local silicon. Specifically, the cdqnK kernel measures **DRAM capacitor-charge decay latency variations** ($\Delta\tau$) and **transistor register thermal noise** ($\eta$) [2, 3]:

$$\text{Raw Key } K_{\text{raw}} = \text{Sweep}(\Delta\tau, \; \eta)$$

Because these variations are unique to each individual chip, the raw key is physically unclonable.

### C. Stabilization via Fuzzy Extractors
To prevent environmental fluctuations (such as temperature spikes or voltage drops) from corrupting the harvested key, we apply a **Fuzzy Extractor** (Helper Data Algorithm) [7]. 

During the onboarding phase, the kernel generates non-sensitive "helper data" $w$, which is stored on-disk. When generating the key on-demand, the fuzzy extractor reads the noisy raw bits, combines them with $w$, and executes a **Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) error-correcting code** [7]:

$$\text{FuzzyExtractor}(K_{\text{raw}}, \; w) \to \pi_{\text{puf}}$$

This error correction guarantees that the resulting physical witness $\pi_{\text{puf}}$ remains perfectly stable and deterministic across extreme operational temperature ranges, while remaining mathematically impossible to clone or emulate on another machine [2, 7].

## III. Direct Anonymous Attestation (DAA) over Sheaves

To prove to the network that the executing hardware is a genuine, certified cdqnOS node without exposing the unique physical witness $\pi_{\text{puf}}$ (which would allow tracking), we implement **Direct Anonymous Attestation (DAA)**.

### A. The Bilinear Pairing
Let $G_1$, $G_2$, and $G_T$ be prime-order abelian groups represented as stalks in the category $\mathbf{CondAb}$ of condensed abelian groups [9, 10]. We define a non-degenerate, computable bilinear map (pairing) $e$ [6]:

$$e: G_1 \times G_2 \to G_T$$

This pairing satisfies the bilinearity condition for any $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$:

$$e(P^a, \; Q^b) = e(P, \; Q)^{ab} \quad \forall P \in G_1, \; Q \in G_2$$

### B. The DAA Functor
We model the DAA protocol as a **Symmetric Monoidal Functor** $\mathcal{F}_{\text{daa}}$ that maps the local physical silicon measurements to this pairing structure:

$$\mathcal{F}_{\text{daa}}: \mathbf{Prof}^{\text{op}} \to \mathbf{CondAb}$$

When a node proposes a state transition, it generates a Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZKP) of the **Syndrome Decoding Problem (SDP)** on-the-fly, utilizing the specific pairing-based variant of the DAA protocol [4, 5] and code-based post-quantum cryptography [12, 13, 14]:
*   The private key for the ZKP is generated dynamically by the physical witness $\pi_{\text{puf}}$, which is immediately scrubbed from registers after use [15].
*   The verifying nodes on the network evaluate the bilinear pairing equations of the group signature. This mathematically proves that the signing node belongs to the "group of genuine, certified cdqnOS hardware nodes," without being able to identify *which specific chip* signed the transaction [4, 5]. 
*   This solves the security-privacy paradox: the execution is fully verified, while the user's sovereign identity remains completely anonymous and untraceable.

## IV. The Adaptive Hardware Fallback Mechanism

To maintain the absolute portability and modularity of the cdqn ecosystem, the physical attestation layer must remain adaptable to virtualized or legacy environments.

Following the formalisms of **Linear Logic** [11] and resource-bounded type systems [16], we utilize the **Resource Functor ($\Phi$)** to dynamically negotiate the local hardware capabilities at bootstrap:

$$\Phi: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{H}$$

**The Native Silicon Path:**  
If executing on native, open-source RISC-V or physical silicon, the functor $\Phi$ maps directly to the physical DRAM latency sweep, generating the witness $\pi_{\text{puf}}$ from the hardware substrate [2, 3].

**The Legacy Virtualization Path:**  
If executing inside a virtualized guest OS (where physical memory timing sweeps are blocked by the hypervisor), the functor $\Phi$ automatically falls back to a standardized, software-bound interface. It harvests entropy from the host OS's provided cryptographic sources (such as a hardware TPM, secure enclave, or WebCrypto API), preserving compatibility and execution safety [1, 2].

## V. [Empirical Horizon]

*While the Physical Unclonable Stalk ($PU\text{-}\mathcal{F}_c$) and the pairing-based DAA functor mathematically guarantee identity-hiding hardware verification, we have only defined this as a static, isolated verification of the executing tool.*

*We have not yet modeled how to transition this identity during physical component replacement (such as hot-swapping a damaged DRAM or CPU module) or how to link this verified tool to a legally accountable human operator. To resolve this, we hypothesize that the system must implement the **Topological Hot-Swap Functor** and the **Human Accountability Anchor ($\mathcal{A}_{\text{human}}$)**, which will be the sole subject of **Chapter 1.11**.*

## VI. Glossary of Terms for Non-Technical Readers

*   **Physical Unclonable Function (PUF):** A hardware security feature that exploits microscopic, sub-micron manufacturing variations in silicon chips to generate unique, uncopyable cryptographic keys on-demand.
*   **Fuzzy Extractor:** An algorithm that uses error-correcting codes to stabilize the noisy, fluctuating raw measurements of a physical hardware chip, ensuring the same identical key is reconstructed every time.
*   **Direct Anonymous Attestation (DAA):** A cryptographic protocol that allows a hardware chip to prove to a remote verifier that it is genuine and certified without revealing its unique identity or serial number.
*   **Bilinear Map (Pairing):** An advanced mathematical mapping between different algebraic groups, used here to construct identity-hiding group signatures for the hardware.
*   **Resource Functor ($\Phi$):** The compiler operator in cdqnOS that maps abstract mathematical contexts to the physical capacity and performance boundaries of the local CPU and memory.
